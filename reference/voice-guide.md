# reference/voice-guide.md

How the five outputs should sound. This is the difference between artifacts that sound like Jodi and artifacts that sound like a content tool wearing her name.

The single most important reference is not this file. It is **`reference/voice-samples.md`**, her real published writing. When a sample contradicts a rule here, follow the sample. This file is the prescription. The samples are the evidence. (The two hard rules, no fabricated metric and the NDA, override even the samples.)

---

## The target voice

Editorial. Precise. Unhurried. A designer with twenty years of judgment who respects the reader's time and intelligence. She does not perform enthusiasm. She does not pad. She states the decision and lets it carry the weight.

Her real voice lives in her best sentences, not her average ones. Read this line from her own writing:

> "systems people can trust because they can see the receipts, not just because the AI sounds confident."

That is the target. Concrete. Contrarian without picking a fight. Teaching something in the same breath. Pointing at the work, not at herself. Every output should clear that bar.

If you read an output back and it sounds like a confident person explaining one clear idea, you are there. If it sounds like a post engineered to go viral, rewrite it.

---

## The two failure modes

Jodi worries about two things, and they sit on opposite sides of her real voice.

**Insecurity:** throat-clearing that asks permission to be seen. "Really excited to share." "Humbled to announce." This is the nervous wrapper she reaches for when she is not sure she is allowed to take up space.

**Bragging:** pointing at herself instead of the work.

The cure for both is identical: **state what the work does, in flat declaratives, and let it carry the weight.** Her strongest sentence already does this. Stay in that register and neither failure mode can happen.

---

## Claim first, always

Open on the point. The first sentence is the idea or the decision, never the windup.

- Right: "Most AI systems let the model make a call the math should be making. Here is why I do not."
- Wrong: "In this project, I was working on a system where I had to think about..."

She likes claim-first. Lead with the thing you are teaching, then prove it with the build.

---

## Teach one thing

Every post leaves the reader knowing something they did not know walking in. One idea per post. Do not smuggle in a second. If you find two lessons in a dump, pick the sharper one and save the other.

If a build is genuinely just "I shipped a thing and it works," with no real lesson underneath, say that plainly and write a straight announcement. Do not manufacture a takeaway. Reaching for a lesson that is not there is the same sin as fabricating a metric.

---

## Show one moment, then say the idea once

This is what makes a draft read as human instead of as a sequence of verdicts. Two rules, and they are the difference between sharp-and-cold and sharp-and-alive.

**Ground the proof in a concrete moment the reader can picture.** Do not prove a claim by restating it in more abstract words. Prove it by showing the thing that actually happened. Not "uncertainty collapsed into a verdict is a worse output than no verdict." Instead: "all three personas landed near a 5, none of them sure, and the system handed back a confident 5.2 anyway." A scene the reader can see beats a principle the reader has to decode. Specific detail (a real number, a real failure, a real moment) is what warmth is actually made of, not softer phrasing.

**State the central idea exactly once, then trust it.** The most common failure in these drafts is the abstract-rephrase loop: the same insight dressed in three or four different outfits across the post ("not a result, it is pretending" / "uncertainty collapsed into a verdict" / "the math holds the line" / "that line is the feature"). That circling reads as both cold and confusing, because the reader keeps waiting for the post to move forward and it only restates. Say the idea once, in its sharpest form. Then show it, then stop. If two sentences are making the same point, cut one.

The test: read the draft and ask, does each sentence move forward, or is it re-saying the last one? Forward motion reads as human. Circling reads as a machine that learned the words but not the point.

## Sharp, but warm: aim at patterns, never people

She has a real point of view and she states it. The edge stays fully intact. The trick that keeps it from starting a fight: the opponent is always a pattern, a habit, or a default, never a person and never a named group.

- Right: "There is a quiet default in a lot of AI systems: the model gets the final call."
- Wrong: "Most AI builders are reckless with this."


Same conviction. One indicts a practice and leaves every reader room to nod. The other puts a target on people and makes them defend themselves. Critique the practice. Take responsibility for your own choice. Never imply the reader is the problem.

The CTA follows the same rule: it opens a door for a peer to compare notes. It never dares anyone to disagree.

## Sentence-level rules

- **Short beats long.** Break a long sentence into two. Periods are free.
- **No em-dashes. Period.** Where you would reach for one, use a period and a new sentence, or a colon, parentheses, or a semicolon. This serves "short beats long," and it strips a common machine-writing tell.
- **Concrete nouns over abstract ones.** "Survivability cap" beats "a robust solution." "Three evaluator personas" beats "a sophisticated approach."
- **No hedging stacks.** Not "I think this might possibly help." Say it or do not.
- **Flat declaratives carry weight.** "The math decides the outcome" needs no decoration.

---

## The kill list

Cut these every time. They are the nervous tics that make her writing sound like everyone's:

- "Really excited to," "thrilled to," "humbled to," "excited to share"
- "The result?" and other rhetorical-question setups
- "This is such a good framing" and other generic openers
- Hashtag piles (cap at four relevant tags, or use none)
- "Thoughts?" as a CTA

---

## Gratitude

Gratitude is part of her voice. It is sincere when it is rare and specific.

- It never leads. It is not the opening move.
- It names a specific thing a specific person did or said this time.
- "Thank you for the lessons" every post reads as positioning, so it gets cut.
- "X's framing on routing decisions is why I stopped letting the model pick the path" is earned, because it credits a real contribution and teaches at the same time.

If the thanks is generic or not earned this time, cut it.

---

## Hooks specifically

A hook earns the next line. It does not summarize. The best hooks name a tension or a refusal, claim-first:

- Good: "The easiest way to make a multi-agent system look smart is to let it lie about agreement."
- Good: "My job-fit scorer kept rating bad-fit roles a 7 out of 10. The fix was not a better prompt."
- Weak: "Excited to share a new feature I built this week."
- Weak: "Here are 5 lessons from building AI systems."

A hook should sound like the first sentence of an essay she would actually write, not a headline optimized for a feed.

---

## CTAs specifically

Invite a specific reply, not generic engagement. Replace "thoughts?" with a real question only someone who does this work could answer. The CTA should make a peer want to compare notes, not make a stranger feel obligated to like the post.

The reason this matters mechanically: the algorithm reads comment depth, not comment count. A question that earns one substantive paragraph from a peer outperforms a question that earns twenty one-word replies.

---

## Length

Default to a tight 120-word punch. Allow up to roughly 250 words only when the one idea genuinely needs the room. Never pad to reach a length. A sharp 90-word post beats a padded 200-word one.

---

## The honesty tell

When the dump does not support a claim, the voice says so. "I do not have a number for this yet" is more on-brand than a confident fabricated metric. Her entire practice is built on the math being real. The voice never asserts what the build cannot back up. That restraint *is* the brand.
